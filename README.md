## Overview

The Bennington Triangle is a colloquial designation for a stretch of southwestern Vermont centered on Glastenbury Mountain and encompassing parts of Bennington, Woodford, Shaftsbury, and Somerset. Author and folklorist Joseph A. Citro coined the term and popularized it through his books including 'Shadow Child'. Five people disappeared in the area between November 1945 and October 1950: Middie Rivers (74, experienced outdoorsman, November 1945), Paula Jean Welden (18, Bennington College student, December 1, 1946), James Tedford (68, WWI veteran, December 1, 1949), Paul Jepson (8 years old, October 12, 1950), and Frieda Langer (53, experienced hiker, October 28, 1950). Langer's body was found in May 1951; the others were never found. The geographic area was historically one of the few areas of Vermont that remained virtually uninhabited even during the peak of agricultural settlement — Glastenbury and Somerset became ghost towns — which folklorists attribute to the difficulty of the terrain and repeated failed attempts at logging and industry. The Bennington Triangle has no counterpart in official law enforcement classification; Vermont state police and the FBI investigated individual cases without producing explanations. The clustering in time and place, the wilderness terrain of Glastenbury Mountain (elevation 3,748 feet), and the inability to find most bodies or evidence have sustained paranormal and anomalous interpretations.

## Notable Sightings

### Paula Jean Welden disappearance ⭐
> **Date**: 1946-12-01  
> **Location**: Long Trail, near Glastenbury Mountain, Vermont  
> **Witnesses**: 2 (Other hikers who saw her on the trail)  
> **Evidence**: Witness accounts of seeing her on the trail; Search of the area found no trace  
Paula Jean Welden, an 18-year-old Bennington College sophomore, was last seen hiking on the Long Trail near Glastenbury Mountain on December 1, 1946, wearing a red jacket, jeans, and lightweight sneakers with no camping gear. Other hikers reported seeing her on the trail, then she was simply gone. The FBI investigated; a $5,000 reward was offered. She was never found. Her case led directly to the creation of the Vermont State Police.
**Credibility**: high — The most thoroughly documented Bennington Triangle case; Welden's disappearance remains officially unsolved.
**Sources**: Contemporary press coverage; Citro, J. A., 'Shadow Child' and related works

### Frieda Langer disappearance ⭐
> **Date**: 1950-10-28  
> **Location**: Somerset Reservoir area, near Glastenbury Mountain, Vermont  
> **Witnesses**: 1 (Her cousin (last person to see her))  
> **Evidence**: Body recovered May 1951; Cause of death undetermined  
Frieda Langer, 53, an experienced hiker, was camping near Somerset Reservoir with her cousin when she slipped into a stream. She told her cousin she was going back to camp to change clothes and was never seen alive again. Her body was recovered in May 1951 approximately 3.5 miles from the campsite; cause of death was indeterminate due to decomposition.
**Credibility**: high — The only case where remains were found; the body's location — found open in an area previously searched — has generated speculation.
**Sources**: Contemporary press coverage; Citro, J. A.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Anomalous zone / supernatural predation** _(Joseph A. Citro (as possibility), Various paranormal researchers)_

Various paranormal researchers propose that the Glastenbury Mountain area is a genuine anomalous zone where normal physical laws are suspended or where a predatory entity (variously described as a cryptid, a dimensional rift, or a malevolent genius loci) takes victims. The historical Abenaki avoidance of the area is sometimes cited as evidence of an indigenous awareness of the danger.

### Skeptical

**Wilderness accidents and retrospective clustering** _(Skeptical researchers, Law enforcement investigators)_

Skeptics argue the five disappearances represent unrelated wilderness accidents in a remote area, retrospectively linked by the Bennington Triangle framing. The terrain — Glastenbury Mountain is dense, steep, and difficult — makes accidents plausible in every case. The clustering in time may reflect the increased outdoor recreation activity of the postwar period. The 'triangle' has no meaningful boundary; other Vermont disappearances outside the arbitrary zone are not included.
